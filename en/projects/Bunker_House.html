Name BUNKER HOUSE
Location Rabat, Maroc
Program Family house
Area 370 m2

000_R1 PUBLI Feuille de travail _ Indépendant.jpg

For a long time a vacant lot, the site of the villa, located south of Rabat, faces a significant vis-à-vis to the west, consisting of a row of houses. Strict urban rules of development apply there. Designed for a couple in their forties with a busy lifestyle and their three children, the villa tries to reconcile their desire for openness and intimacy, combining convivial spaces for entertaining friends and family and others offering the possibility of peace and quiet. In alignment with the street, the villa has two monolithic and mute volumes. Once past the fence and the altimetric games of the garden, this silent architecture reveals open and sheltered spaces.

Compact, the house is organized on three levels. On the garden level, an atrium serves the vertical circulation, a kitchen, a living room and a generous living space. Open 180° to the garden, it extends through large sliding windows to two terraces to the east and west. Like courtyards, these terraces are real additional spaces sheltered from the summer heat and the Atlantic trade winds and animated by glimpses of the sky through large oculi. The first floor is divided into two wings on either side of the atrium. On the one hand, the master suite installed within the levitating monolith and opening onto a vegetated terrace to the south and an east-facing patio dedicated to the bathroom space. On the other hand, three bedrooms each with a private bathroom and a planted balcony.

Finally, thanks to the raising of the garden level, a third level discreetly opens onto an English courtyard planted in a semi-basement. The atrium plays a key role in the spatial organization of the villa. It articulates and connects all levels. Its spectacular volume, magnified by natural zenithal lighting, offers many visual porosities and remains unsuspected from the outside. Contrasting with the horizontality of the living room, the verticality of the atrium allows, in the event of a heat wave, to play a role as a "wind tower" and to naturally ventilate and cool the heart of the house. The bold articulation of sculptural volumes is counterbalanced by simple and raw materials: matte white wash on plastered concrete, veined walnut wood, bouchardé Taza stone and patinated raw steel. This deliberately restricted palette is complemented by touches of sky and vegetation typical of the Moroccan Atlantic coast that can be found at all levels inside and outside.
